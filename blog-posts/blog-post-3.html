The Basis Trap: Why 20-Year Owners Can't Sell Without a Massive Tax Bill

Key Takeaways for Long-Term Owners

  • What Cost Basis Is and Why It Traps You
  • The 1031 Exchange Escape Hatch
  • Delaware Statutory Trusts and the Passive Path
  • Installment Sales, Opportunity Zones, and the Step-Up

What Cost Basis Is and Why It Traps You

Cost basis is simply the number the IRS uses to measure your gain on a sale. It starts with what you paid for the property, gets increased by capital improvements, and gets reduced every year by depreciation. For an owner who bought a Chicagoland warehouse in 2005 for $2 million and has depreciated it for 20 years, the adjusted basis today might sit closer to $600,000. If that same building sells for $6 million, the taxable gain is not $4 million—it is $5.4 million once depreciation recapture is added back in. Between federal capital gains at 20 percent, depreciation recapture at 25 percent, the 3.8 percent net investment income tax, and Illinois state tax at 4.95 percent, the total bill can eat 30 to 35 percent of the sale price. That is the trap: the longer you hold, the lower your basis drops, and the more painful a clean sale becomes.


The 1031 Exchange Escape Hatch

The most common way out of the basis trap is a Section 1031 like-kind exchange, which lets an owner defer the entire tax bill by rolling the proceeds into another investment property. The rules are strict: the seller has 45 days from closing to identify replacement properties and 180 days to close on them, and the funds must be held by a qualified intermediary the entire time. The replacement property must be equal or greater in value and debt to avoid "boot," which is the portion that gets taxed. For a Chicagoland owner sitting on a low-basis strip center, a 1031 into a triple-net retail portfolio or a small warehouse can preserve every dollar of equity for the next deal. The catch is timing—45 days moves fast in a tight market, and identifying the wrong property can force a taxable sale by default.


Delaware Statutory Trusts and the Passive Path

Many long-term owners are tired of tenants, toilets, and trash but still cannot stomach the tax hit of a straight sale. A Delaware Statutory Trust, or DST, solves both problems at once. A DST is a pre-packaged fractional interest in institutional-grade real estate—often distribution centers, medical office, or multifamily—that qualifies as replacement property under Section 1031. The owner sells the active property, 1031s the proceeds into one or more DST interests, and steps into fully passive ownership with monthly distributions and no management responsibility. Typical DST holds run seven to ten years, and at the end the sponsor either sells the asset or offers another 1031 into a new DST. For an owner in their 60s or 70s who wants income without the headaches, DSTs are one of the cleanest exits available.


Installment Sales, Opportunity Zones, and the Step-Up

When a 1031 is not practical, there are still ways to soften the blow. An installment sale under Section 453 lets the seller carry back a note and spread the gain—and the tax—over the years the buyer makes payments, which can keep the seller in a lower bracket each year. Qualified Opportunity Zone funds allow an owner to defer gain until 2026 year-end and, if held for ten years, eliminate tax on the new investment's appreciation entirely. The most powerful tool of all, however, requires patience: the step-up in basis at death. Under current law, when an owner passes away, the property's basis resets to fair market value, and heirs can sell the next day with essentially zero capital gains tax. For owners over 70 with strong estate planning, holding until death and leaving the property to heirs is often the most tax-efficient exit of all.


How Owners Can Actually Get Out

The basis trap is real, and it is the single biggest reason so many quality Chicagoland industrial and retail buildings never come to market. Owners who bought in the 1990s or early 2000s look at a sale and see 30 percent of their equity walking out the door, so they simply hold—even when the property no longer fits their life. But the tools to escape the trap do exist. A 1031 exchange preserves every dollar for the next active deal. A DST turns an active property into passive income without triggering tax. An installment sale spreads the pain over years. An Opportunity Zone fund can wipe out future gains entirely. And for owners late in their careers, a step-up in basis at death can zero out decades of deferred tax in a single moment.

The point is not that selling is impossible—it is that selling badly is what costs owners the most. The right structure, chosen years before the deal, is what separates a 35 percent tax hit from a fully deferred exit. That planning conversation is worth having long before the property is ever listed.
