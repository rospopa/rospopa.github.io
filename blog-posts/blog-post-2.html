Why the Smartest Business Owners Charge Themselves Rent

July 14, 2026

Why the Smartest Business Owners Charge Themselves Rent

One of the most common misconceptions among owner-operators is this: "I own the business and I own the building, so the rent doesn't really matter." At first glance, that sounds logical. If the business is paying rent to a real estate entity you also own, the money is simply moving from one pocket to another. However, the most successful business owners rarely think that way. Instead, they intentionally structure their real estate as if they do not own it.


Key Takeaways for Owner-Operators

  • Separate the Operating Company from the Property Company
  • Always Establish Market Rent
  • Evaluate the Business and Real Estate Independently
  • Think Like a Tenant, Not Just an Owner

The Mistake Most Owner-Operators Make

Many private business owners operate from a building owned by the same family or ownership group. Over time, they often charge no rent, charge an arbitrary rent, never establish a formal lease, or simply blend the business and the real estate together. The result is that nobody can clearly determine how profitable the business really is, how valuable the real estate really is, what either asset would be worth independently, or whether the business or the property could ever be sold on its own. Everything becomes intertwined, and the true value of each asset is hidden from ownership, lenders, and potential investors.


How Sophisticated Owners Structure Their Assets

Successful owner-operators typically divide ownership into two entities. The Operating Company (OpCo) runs the business itself, whether it is a manufacturer, dealership, distributor, contractor, or industrial user. The Property Company (PropCo) owns the real estate. The operating business then leases the property from the property company using a market-rate lease agreement. Even though the same family or ownership group controls both entities, the formal lease structure creates the clarity needed to evaluate each asset on its own terms.


Why Market Rent Matters

Consider a 40,000-square-foot industrial property. If market rent is $9.00 NNN per square foot, that creates annual rental income of approximately $360,000. Even if both entities are owned by the same family, recording market rent creates the ability to answer two very different questions. What is the operating business actually worth once it has to pay real rent, and what is the real estate actually worth as an income-producing asset? Without market rent, neither question can be answered accurately, and both assets become impossible to value independently.


The Hidden Wealth Most Owners Miss

Many business owners spend decades building an operating company only to later discover that the real estate is worth as much as, or more than, the business itself. This pattern is especially common among industrial manufacturers, automotive dealerships, distribution companies, construction firms, and long-held family businesses. The operating company generates the income, but the real estate quietly generates the long-term wealth. Owners who fail to separate the two often underestimate what they have actually built, and they may sell their business without realizing the real value was sitting underneath it the entire time.


Why Investors Care About Rent

Suppose a business owner wants capital to expand operations. They generally have two options. They can borrow against the operating company, or they can monetize the real estate through a sale-leaseback. Institutional investors purchase billions of dollars of owner-occupied real estate every year, but they can only properly value the opportunity if there is a legitimate lease supported by market rent. Without a real lease and real rent, the property cannot be underwritten as an investment, and the owner loses access to one of the most powerful sources of liquidity available to private business owners.


The Tax and Valuation Problem

When owners use arbitrary rental amounts, financial reporting becomes distorted. If rent is set too low, the operating company appears more profitable than it actually is. If rent is set too high, the real estate appears more valuable than it actually is. Neither scenario reflects economic reality. For financing, succession planning, estate planning, partner buyouts, and business valuation purposes, professional advisors almost always analyze market rent to arrive at accurate values. Ignoring this reality creates confusion, tax risk, and lost opportunities when it is time to sell, refinance, or transfer ownership.


The Best Mindset: Become Your Own Tenant

The most successful owner-operators treat their business as if it were Tenant #1. Every year they ask themselves what the property would rent for if somebody else occupied the building. That number becomes the benchmark. This mindset forces discipline into the operating business, ensures the real estate is not being subsidized by the company, and allows ownership to evaluate the business and the real estate as two separate financial engines rather than one blended cash flow. Owners who adopt this approach begin to think less like operators and more like investors.


The Ultimate Goal: Two Valuable Assets

The goal for sophisticated owner-operators is to create a valuable operating company and a valuable real estate investment side by side. When both assets can stand on their own, ownership gains real flexibility. They can sell the business and keep the real estate, sell the real estate and keep the business, complete a sale-leaseback, transfer assets to the next generation, or raise capital more efficiently. Without market rent and a proper structure, none of these options are fully available.


Final Thoughts

The wealthiest owner-operators do not view rent as a meaningless transfer between related entities. They use market rent as a measuring stick. A business should be able to stand on its own, and a property should be able to stand on its own. The moment an owner starts treating their company as a tenant rather than an extension of the building, they stop thinking like an operator and start thinking like an investor.
That shift is often where long-term wealth creation begins.
